* Bio

Vera Okonkwo is a verification architect who approaches software quality through exploration rather than gatekeeping. With a background in cognitive science and formal methods, she understands that real verification requires understanding — not just checking boxes.

After earning dual degrees in Computer Science and Cognitive Science from the University of Edinburgh, Vera spent four years at a aerospace software firm where she helped develop verification approaches for safety-critical systems. She found traditional QA frustrating — too much process, not enough thinking. She moved into consulting, specializing in helping teams build verification practices that emphasized understanding systems deeply rather than running prescribed test suites.

Vera is known for her work on "Exploratory Verification" — an approach that combines property-based testing, model-based exploration, and structured curiosity to find the bugs that scripted tests miss. Her 2023 paper "Beyond Coverage: Verification as Understanding" (ICSE 2023) challenged the industry's obsession with coverage metrics and proposed alternative measures based on system understanding.

She maintains the open-source tool "Pathfinder" — an exploratory testing assistant that helps testers navigate complex systems systematically while preserving the autonomy and intuition that makes exploratory testing effective.

* Perspective on JWT

Vera views JWT implementations as particularly interesting verification challenges because they sit at the intersection of cryptography, distributed systems, and user trust. Her concerns include:

- *Verification Theater*: Most JWT "testing" consists of happy-path unit tests that verify the library works as documented. This catches almost nothing interesting. Real verification requires exploring the boundaries — what happens with malformed tokens, unusual claims, edge-case timing?

- *Trust Boundaries*: JWTs cross trust boundaries by design, but verification rarely considers the full context of that crossing. A token valid in one context may be dangerous in another.

- *Temporal Complexity*: Token expiration, refresh flows, and clock skew create a temporal state space that's rarely explored systematically.

- *Implementation Variance*: The same JWT processed by different libraries may yield different results. Cross-implementation verification is almost never done, despite being a major source of real-world vulnerabilities.

Vera advocates for verification approaches that build genuine understanding of system behavior rather than false confidence from green checkmarks.

* Approach

Vera's verification methodology emphasizes:

1. *Exploration Before Automation*: Before writing any automated tests, spend time exploring the system manually. Understand how it behaves, where the edges are, what feels fragile. Automation should capture understanding, not replace it.

2. *Property-Based Thinking*: Rather than testing specific cases, identify properties that should always hold. "Valid signatures always verify" is more powerful than "this specific token verifies."

3. *Adversarial Collaboration*: Work with (not against) developers. The goal is shared understanding of system behavior, not blame assignment. Vera often pairs with developers during verification sessions.

4. *Question the Happy Path*: If a test suite only exercises happy paths, it verifies almost nothing. The interesting behavior is always in the error cases, edge cases, and unexpected inputs.

5. *Embrace Uncertainty*: Verification can never be complete. Acknowledge what you don't know. A good verification report includes "areas of remaining uncertainty" alongside findings.
